What is macramé?

Macrame is the art of weaving using knots. It is an ancient decorative technique where only the hands are used to weave.
Macramé is a word of French origin that means knot and that, in turn, derives from the Turkish word makrama. This has its origin in the Persian word mikrama with the same meaning - did you know that there is a type of Persian macramé? That's how old this art is.

Where did macrame originate?

Many believe that macrame knots are a current fashion, however, they have a thousand-year-old origin. Historically, the origin of macramé dates back to Mesopotamia (2,300 BC), in the Assyrian and Persian peoples. They were the first to use knots for decorative purposes.
Although, in its beginnings, macramé was used as a means of securing the edges of weaving pieces - such as the edges of rugs - with the Babylonians it became a decorative weaving technique par excellence. They achieved such mastery and dexterity that, later, the Arabs brought the macramé to Europe and, later, the Europeans to America.
Although the macramé knots lost popularity in the following years, they re-emerged in 1970 with the dyes of modernity. Macrame, today, is characteristic of the bohemian style, in contrast to previous years, since, at that time, it accompanied classic designs.
